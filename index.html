Basic TEI Elements for Encoding Primary Manuscripts in Shelley-Godwin Archive

by David Brookshire

1. Zones

The manuscript page is divided into zones. The "main" zone contains the main body of writing. The "pagination" zone contains page numbers supplied by the author. The "library" zone contains additional information, such as folio numbers, supplied by the holding library. The "top" zone contains chapter headings or titles. And the "left-margin" zone indicates that a margin has been created on the left side of the page using a vertical pencil line. For the Frankenstein manuscript, the left margin zone is used as a space for additions. The differing characteristics of manuscript sources will determine what kinds of zones will be used.

Here, the "m" and the "ra" have been struckthrough without any other modifications. The most common deletion type is a strikethrough. Less common forms of deletion include ink smears and overwrites. Because overwritten text includes both an addition and a deletion, it would be encoded within a element that links them together as a single authorial intervention. For example,

The "is" in "this" has been written over with an "e" to make "the."

10. Shifts in Hands

To indicate an addition in a hand other than that of the primary author, use the "hand" attribute within the element.

string and. The backslash indicates that the element is empty and that the shift in hand continues until another shift in hand takes place.

11. Uncertain Readings

Text that is illegible is enclosed within the element. If the text is partially recognizable, indicate it as follows:

During her confinement mady many

If the text is obscured by ink blots, foxing, or is completely illegible. Use an empty tag and indicate the approximate length of the uncertain reading. "char" stands for "characters".

During her confinement many

12. Encoding Spaces

To encode significant spaces between passages or words, use the element and indicate the length of the space.

acquired and

Do not encode spaces unless there is a noticeable gap that extends well beyond the normal variation of spaces within a handwritten manuscript.

13. Damage to the Manuscript

If text is illegible or missing from manuscript because of damage, such as being torn or cut away, use the element. The reviewer will add more details to the encoding.

15. Paratextual Notations and Additions

For paratextual notations such as carets, brackets, and "X"s that indicate insertion points for added material, enclose them within the element.

For notes or instructions added by either the author or compositor, use the element. For example, in the fair-copy draft for Frankenstein, the compositor writes his initials and indicates where he either ends or begins typesetting:

F. W./My courage . . .
